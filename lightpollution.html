What is Light Pollution?

Have you ever been in a city or well-lit area at night and looked up? How many stars can you see?
Depending on where you are, you might see just a handful - perhaps only the brightest ones.

All the bright lights we have: streetlights, cars, and other night-time lighting, play a large part in the drowning of our night.
Light pollution occurs when light escapes upward and scatters across the atmosphere. This results in the "glow" you see
when looking at a city or town at night. Called sky glow, this dome of light drowns out the natural darkness of the night,
resulting in a forever-bright night.

Light pollution affects far more than just our night skies. Our health and wellbeing, the environment, ecosystems, and more
are negatively impacted by light pollution.

Light Pollution on Our Skies

Light pollution heavily impacts our night skies. In a dark, unpolluted area, you might see thousands of stars glittering above. However, in a bright, light-polluted sky, you might see only the brightest ones. More than 80% of Americans currently live under skies too bright to see the Milky Way [Source 2].

Just how dark are your skies? We measure the darkness of our night using the Bortle Dark-Sky Scale; this scale measures the magnitude, or brightness, of the night sky and stars [Source 3]. A Class 1 location hosts some of our darkest skies, while a Class 9 location includes some of our largest and brightest cities.

Anyone can see under what Bortle class skies they live in!

Light Pollution Affects Us All

Light pollution affects far more than just our night skies.
All organisms, including plants and animals, have a circadian rhythm: the regulatory physical, mental, and behavioral change over the course of a 24-hour cycle (NIGMS SOURCE). Our circadian rhythm typically follows the day/night cycle.

Excessive nighttime light, however, interferes with this rhythm. Melatonin, the hormone that regulates our sleep-wake cycle, is usually released after the onset of darkness, or night-time. (SOURCE) Increased night-time exposure to light has shown to lower melatonin production, resulting in various mental and physical issues such as fatigue, headaches, and anxiety.

On the Economy

Environmental Light Pollution

Birds

Light pollution negatively impacts our environment and ecosystems. Various animals, particularly those who migrate seasonally, are directly affected by our glowing skies.

Birds rely heavily on the moon to navigate the night, turtle hatchlings use darkness to find the sea, and many other animals, such as frogs, bats, rodents, etc. have their mating, feeding, or nocturnal rhythms disrubted. [SOURCE]

Roughly 200 migratory bird species fly at night over North America and many of these birds fly over brightly-lit regions. The U.S. Fish and Wildlife service estimates five-to-fifty million birds are killed due to collisions with brightly-lit surfaces annually. [SOURCE]

1. Turn off lights when not in use!
2. Close the blinds and drapes at night!
3. Shield your outdoor lights!
4. Avoid blue lights at night!
5. Use LED lights!
