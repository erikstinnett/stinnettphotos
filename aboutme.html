About Me

My name is Erik Stinnett and I am a Computer Science student at the University of Central Arkansas with a love for the night sky!
I've been into astronomy for as long as I remember: I remember getting a book about the Hubble Space Telescope as a kid and being absolutely in awe of the beautiful photos that the Hubble took.



My first dip into astrophotography was on a chilly October night in 2019 when I took out my Meade LX200 and attempted to take photos of the moon by pressing my phone against the eyepiece.
The results, as one could expect, were blurry and out of focus.
This night, however, triggered an interest in photographing the night sky. I've spent much of my time since investing in astrophotography, learning, practicing, and, of course, learning some more!

The Dark Side of Lighting

The Dark Side of Lighting is the culmination of two years of work. As a student of the University of Central Arkansas' Schedler Honors College, I get to complete a Capstone project that I have spent multiple days (and nights) working on.

As an astrophotographer, the issue of light pollution strikes close to what I love doing on a nightly basis. I felt that light pollution and its effects on our planet is not widely known nor talked about and I wanted to make a change. The Dark Side of Lighting is meant to do just that: spread awareness of the issue of light pollution and the brightening of our nights.

This Capstone project combines two disciplines, astrophotography and web development, in order to spread the message. By utilizing 360-degree panoramas I aim to visualize the impact that artificial light has on our skies in the best way that I know how. I've taken two panoramas at Steel Creek, on the Buffalo National River, a dark-sky site, and am juxtaposing them with two panoramas taken at Harris Brake Lake, a light-polluted region. These panoramas will be displayed at the Griffin Planetarium on UCA campus during my Capstone presentation on April 27th.

As a Computer Science student, I wanted my Capstone to represent something that I could see myself doing as a career. I wrote this website using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, learning so much on the way. I wanted to use this website to be not only my personal photography portfolio but also as a means to host the 360-degree panoramas along with information about light pollution on an online platform.

My Equipment

Deep-Space Photography

The Esprit 100ED

I use two telescopes for deep-space astrophotography, an Esprit 100ed and a Meade LX200 8". I rotate all other equipment between the two.

Mount: Skywatcher EQ6-R Pro
Camera: ZWO ASI1600MM Pro Monochrome
Autoguider: ZWO ASI120MM Mini
Filter Wheel: ZWO 5-posiiton EFW
Focuser: ZWO EAF
Connected using a ZWO ASIAIR

The Esprit's focal length of 550 millimeters makes it a great choice for wider-field astrophotography or of larger targets such as various nebulae.

The Meade LX200 8" GPS

The Meade Lx200 8" GPS telescope is a great option for planetary or up-close lunar photography. Its focal length of 2000 millimeters allows me to get a more magnified view of my target.

Milkyway Photography

The Sony A7IV

For milkyway and nightscape photography, I use a much lighter and more portable set-up. Most of my photos have been taken using a Canon T2i and a Sigma 14mm lens. However, as of November 2022, I've since upgraded to a Sony A7IV and a Sony 14mm lens.

Camera: Sony A7IV, Canon EOS Rebel T2i
Lens: Sony 14mm f1.8, Sigma 14mm f1.8

The Observatory

My Roll-off Roof Observatory

Between 2021 and 2022 we built a roll-off roof observatory in our backyard. This set-up allows me to leave my gear up permanently instead of dragging it in and out of the garage every day. The observatory also allows me to not have to keep an eye on it every night, as it is behind a locked door and four walls.

Thanks to the ASIAIR and remote desktop, I am able to control my telescope from anywhere I want, meaning I've been able to do astrophotography while I've been away for college.
